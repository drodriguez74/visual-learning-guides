// Process Improvement Framework

Lean &
Six Sigma

Two of the most powerful methodologies for eliminating waste, reducing variation, and delivering consistent quality at scale.

Lean

Eliminate Waste

Originating from the Toyota Production System, Lean focuses on delivering maximum value to the customer by systematically identifying and removing non-value-adding activities (waste) from every process.

Six Sigma

Reduce Variation

Developed at Motorola and refined by GE, Six Sigma uses statistical methods and a structured problem-solving approach (DMAIC) to reduce defects and process variation to near-perfect levels.

Lean Six Sigma combines both: Lean speeds up processes by removing waste, Six Sigma improves quality by reducing variation. Together they deliver fast, consistent, high-quality outcomes.

// Toyota Production System

Lean
Principles

The 5 core principles of Lean define a cycle of continuous improvement focused entirely on customer value.

1

Identify Value

Define value strictly from the customer's perspective. Value is anything the customer is willing to pay for. Everything else is waste.

Voice of Customer (VOC) CTQ Trees Kano Model
2

Map the Value Stream

Chart every step in the process from raw material to customer delivery. Identify which steps add value, which are necessary waste, and which are pure waste to eliminate.

Value Stream Map (VSM) Swimlane Diagrams SIPOC
3

Create Flow

Remove bottlenecks and interruptions so that the remaining value-creating steps flow smoothly and continuously. Eliminate batching where possible.

Kanban Heijunka (Load Leveling) One-Piece Flow
4

Establish Pull

Produce only what the customer demands, when they demand it. This replaces push-based systems (make-to-forecast) with pull-based ones (make-to-order), reducing inventory and overproduction.

Just-in-Time (JIT) Kanban Cards Takt Time
5

Pursue Perfection

Lean is never "done." Once waste is removed, new wastes become visible. The pursuit of perfection (Kaizen) is a perpetual, iterative cycle embedded in the culture.

Kaizen Events PDCA Cycle Gemba Walks
Key Concepts
Takt Time = Available production time ÷ Customer demand rate. This is the "heartbeat" of production — the pace at which you must complete one unit to meet demand without over- or under-producing.
Gemba Walk — "Go to where the work happens." Leaders physically observe the actual process rather than relying on reports. Waste is often only visible on the floor.
The Three MU's — Beyond TIMWOODS

The Toyota Production System recognizes three broader categories of loss. TIMWOODS covers Muda in detail, but Mura and Muri are equally important root causes.

Muda

Waste

Activities that consume resources without adding customer value. The 8 wastes (TIMWOODS) are all forms of Muda — the most visible of the three MU's.

Mura

Unevenness

Irregular demand or uneven workload — e.g., month-end spikes or large batches. Mura causes alternating overburden and idle time, creating both Muri and Muda downstream.

Muri

Overburden

Pushing people, equipment, or processes beyond natural capacity. Signs: workers skipping steps, machines skipping maintenance. Leads to breakdowns, errors, and burnout.

// TIMWOODS Mnemonic

The 8 Wastes

Lean identifies 8 categories of non-value-adding activity. Use the mnemonic TIMWOODS to remember them all.

🚛

T — Transport

Unnecessary movement of materials, products, or information between locations

📦

I — Inventory

Excess raw material, WIP, or finished goods beyond what's immediately needed

🚶

M — Motion

Unnecessary physical movement of people — reaching, walking, searching

⏳

W — Waiting

Idle time when people, equipment, or processes wait for the next step

🏭

O — Overproduction

Producing more, earlier, or faster than customer demand requires — the worst waste

⚙️

O — Over-processing

Doing more work, steps, or quality checks than the customer actually requires

🔧

D — Defects

Errors requiring rework, scrap, or inspection — direct cost and time waste

🧠

S — Skills

Underutilizing people's knowledge, creativity, and experience (the 8th waste)

Overproduction is considered the worst waste in Lean because it generates all other wastes — more inventory, more transport, more motion, more potential defects.
Interview tip: When asked about a process improvement you've made, frame your answer using one or more of these 8 wastes. E.g., "We had significant waiting waste in our API approval workflow, so we implemented async processing to reduce lead time by 40%."
// Statistical Process Control

Six
Sigma

Sigma (σ) measures process variation. Six Sigma means your process produces fewer than 3.4 defects per million opportunities — essentially perfect.

// Sigma Level → Defects Per Million Opportunities (DPMO)
The 1.5σ Shift — The 3.4 DPMO figure assumes a 1.5σ long-term drift in the process mean (Motorola's empirical finding that real processes shift over time). A mathematically perfect 6σ process with no drift would produce just 0.002 DPMO. This shift is why sigma levels in practice use adjusted DPMO tables. Knowing this detail signals genuine depth in an interview.
A 1-sigma process produces ~690,000 defects per million. Moving to 6-sigma reduces this to just 3.4 DPMO — a 200,000× improvement.
Core Concepts
Statistical

Cp, Cpk, Pp & Ppk

Cp measures how well a process fits within spec limits — assuming it's perfectly centered.
Cpk accounts for centering (off-center = lower Cpk). Cpk ≥ 1.33 = capable; Cpk = 2.0 = Six Sigma.

Pp/Ppk are the performance equivalents — same formulas but using overall standard deviation (long-term, all sources of variation) instead of within-subgroup variation. Cp/Cpk reveal process potential; Pp/Ppk reveal actual performance. A large gap between Cpk and Ppk means significant between-subgroup variation exists.

Statistical

DPMO

Defects Per Million Opportunities. Formula: (Defects / (Units × Opportunities per unit)) × 1,000,000. This normalizes defect rates across different process complexities, enabling apples-to-apples comparisons.

Concept

Common vs Special Cause

Common cause variation is random, inherent in the process — addressed by redesigning the system. Special cause variation is assignable to a specific event or factor — addressed by finding and eliminating that root cause.

Concept

CTQ — Critical to Quality

The specific, measurable characteristics of a product or service that directly satisfy customer needs. Translating vague VOC statements ("fast delivery") into measurable CTQs ("shipped within 24 hours") is foundational to Six Sigma projects.

Two Pathways
Existing Process

DMAIC

Define → Measure → Analyze → Improve → Control. Used when you have an existing process that is underperforming. The most common Six Sigma methodology.

New Process

DMADV / DFSS

Define → Measure → Analyze → Design → Verify. Used when designing a new product, service, or process from scratch — or when an existing process is so broken it needs full replacement. Also called Design for Six Sigma (DFSS).

// Six Sigma Methodology

DMAIC

The 5-phase problem-solving framework for improving an existing process. Data-driven and structured — every phase produces measurable outputs and ends with a formal tollgate review.

D

Define

Clearly articulate the problem, project scope, customer requirements, and business impact. Build a Project Charter with goals, timeline, team, and financial benefits. Translate VOC into measurable CTQs.

Project Charter SIPOC Diagram VOC / CTQ Tree Stakeholder Analysis
M

Measure

Establish a baseline. Collect data on the current process performance and validate that your measurement system is reliable (Gage R&R). Without valid measurement, everything downstream is suspect.

Process Map Data Collection Plan Gage R&R Baseline Sigma Level Control Charts
A

Analyze

Identify the root cause(s) of defects or variation. Use data and statistical tools — not opinions. Separate correlation from causation. The goal: verify which X's (inputs) are driving the problem Y (output).

Fishbone / Ishikawa 5 Whys Pareto Chart Regression Analysis Hypothesis Testing FMEA
I

Improve

Design, pilot, and implement solutions that address the root causes. Use experiments to validate that improvements work before full-scale rollout. Quantify the improvement with data.

DOE (Design of Experiments) Pilot Studies Kaizen Events Mistake-Proofing (Poka-Yoke) Solution Matrix
C

Control

Lock in the gains. Document new procedures, train staff, and implement monitoring systems so the process doesn't regress. Hand off ownership to the process owner with a control plan.

Control Chart (SPC) Control Plan SOPs / Work Instructions Visual Management Response Plan
Tollgate Reviews
Tollgate Reviews occur at the end of each DMAIC phase. The project team presents deliverables to the Champion and sponsor, who must formally approve before the team advances. Tollgates enforce rigor, surface risks early, and give leadership visibility and control over project direction. Failing a tollgate means going back and completing the phase — not skipping ahead.
Key Formula
Y = f(X) — The fundamental Six Sigma equation. The output (Y) is a function of the inputs (X's). DMAIC is the journey of discovering which X's matter most, and controlling them.
// Practitioner Toolkit

Key Tools

These are the most commonly tested and referenced tools in Lean Six Sigma. Know what each does and when to use it.

Lean Tools

Value Stream Map (VSM)

Visualizes the entire flow of materials and information through a process from supplier to customer. Highlights waste, bottlenecks, and handoff delays. Current-state maps reveal problems; future-state maps define the target.

Use in: Define / Measure

5S Methodology

Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain. A workplace organization system that reduces waste through visual order and discipline. Often the first improvement implemented in a new Lean program.

Use in: Improve

Kanban

Visual scheduling system using cards or boards to signal production needs, limit WIP, and expose bottlenecks. Core to pull-based systems. WIP limits force the team to finish before starting new work.

Use in: Improve / Control

Poka-Yoke

Mistake-proofing. Designing the process so errors are impossible or immediately obvious — e.g., a USB plug that only fits one way, or a form that won't submit without required fields.

Use in: Improve

A3 Report

A structured one-page problem-solving document (named after A3 paper size) from Toyota. Sections: background → current condition → goal → root cause → countermeasures → follow-up. Forces disciplined thinking in a concise format.

Use in: Define / Analyze / Improve

Heijunka (Level Scheduling)

Production leveling — distributing work evenly across time rather than processing in large batches. Smooths demand variation, reduces Mura (unevenness), and prevents downstream overburden.

Use in: Improve
Six Sigma Tools

SIPOC

Suppliers → Inputs → Process → Outputs → Customers. A high-level process map used at the start of a project to define scope and key stakeholders. Prevents scope creep by agreeing on boundaries early.

Use in: Define

Fishbone Diagram

Also called Ishikawa or Cause-and-Effect diagram. Organizes potential root causes into categories: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Environment (6M's). Best used in a team brainstorm before prioritizing with data.

Use in: Analyze

Pareto Chart

Bar chart ordered by frequency/impact, with a cumulative line. Demonstrates the 80/20 rule — 80% of problems typically come from 20% of causes. Prioritizes focus so teams tackle the highest-impact root causes first.

Use in: Analyze

Control Chart (SPC)

Time-series plot with Upper/Lower Control Limits (UCL/LCL) at ±3σ. Used to distinguish special-cause from common-cause variation and monitor process stability over time.

Use in: Measure / Control

FMEA

Failure Mode & Effects Analysis. Proactively scores potential failures by Severity × Occurrence × Detection = Risk Priority Number (RPN). Prioritizes what to mistake-proof or monitor. Higher RPN = higher priority.

Use in: Analyze / Improve

5 Whys

Repeatedly asking "why?" to drill through symptoms to the true root cause. Simple but powerful — best used after a fishbone to investigate the highest-priority branch. Stop when you reach something you can control.

Use in: Analyze

Gage R&R

Gauge Repeatability & Reproducibility. Measures how much of your observed variation comes from the measurement system itself, not the process. Repeatability = same operator, same instrument variation. Reproducibility = variation between operators. A measurement system contributing >30% of total variation is unacceptable — fix measurement before drawing conclusions.

Use in: Measure

DOE — Design of Experiments

Systematically varies multiple input factors simultaneously to understand their individual and combined effects on the output (Y). Far more efficient than one-factor-at-a-time testing. Key terms: factors, levels, runs, full factorial vs. fractional factorial, interaction effects.

Use in: Improve
