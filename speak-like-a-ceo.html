The Kill List

These phrases aren't wrong — they're invisible. They signal you haven't thought about how you're opening the conversation. High-value communicators never waste the first 10 seconds on autopilot small talk.

❌  Dead Phrase
✓  What to say instead
"How was your weekend?"
Triggers a scripted, forgettable response. Neither party is engaged.
→
"Did you get a chance to decompress at all this weekend?"
Signals awareness of their workload. Invites a real answer.
"How are you?" / "How've you been?"
Purely reflexive. Nobody answers honestly. It's verbal wallpaper.
→
"What's been occupying most of your energy lately?"
Opens a real conversation. Shows you want signal, not noise.
"How's the weather over there?"
Filler. Betrays a lack of curiosity or preparation.
→
"I know you're in [city] — is that still your base, or has that changed?"
Personal, specific, shows you retained context about them.
"So… yeah." / "Anyways…"
Signals you've run out of content. Kills conversational momentum.
→
"What I'm most curious about from your side is—"
Deliberate pivot that keeps you in the driver's seat.
"Does that make sense?"
Undermines your own point. Signals you doubt your clarity.
→
"I'd be curious where you'd push back on that."
Confident. Invites engagement without apologizing for your idea.
"I just wanted to…" / "Sorry to bother you…"
Apologetic framing depreciates everything that follows.
→
"I wanted to get your perspective on something specific."
Direct. Values their time. Respects both parties.
"That's a great question!"
Filler flattery. Overused. Makes you sound like a customer service agent.
→
[Pause] "The honest answer is—" [then answer]
Silence + directness signals confidence. No performance required.
"To be honest with you…"
Implies you're usually not honest. Red flag phrasing.
→
"My candid read on this is—"
Still signals directness, without implying prior deception.

CEO Communication Framework
01
Lead with Insight, Not Information
Never open with facts. Open with your interpretation of facts. "The numbers are in — and what they tell me is..." signals a thinker, not a reporter.
02
Ask Better Questions Than They Expect
The quality of your questions signals the depth of your thinking. One incisive question outweighs ten correct answers. It shows you've already processed the basics.
03
Name the Tension in the Room
Executives trust people who say what others are thinking. "I want to name something that may be uncomfortable" instantly elevates your perceived authority.
04
Speak in Threes
Structure everything in three: "My read on this is X. The risk I see is Y. The move I'd recommend is Z." It sounds complete, disciplined, and memorable.
05
Disagree with Precision
"I'd push back on one thing" is more powerful than "I disagree." It signals surgical thinking. You're not rejecting — you're improving.
06
Close Every Exchange with Intent
Never let a conversation just end. "What I'm taking from this is X — and I'll follow up on Y by Z date." Closes the loop. Signals execution mindset.
Power Phrase Bank
To Open With Authority
"My perspective on this, having thought about it, is—"
"The question I keep coming back to is—"
"Let me share where my head is, and I'd value your reaction."
"There are three things I want to flag before we go further."
To Show Active Intelligence
"What I find most interesting about what you just said is—"
"The implication of that, if I'm following correctly, is—"
"Help me understand the reasoning behind that decision."
"What are we not saying in this room that we probably should be?"
To Push Back Without Conflict
"I want to offer a different frame on that—"
"I'd push back on one assumption embedded in that."
"That may be right — here's where I'd want more evidence."
"I hear the logic. Here's where it breaks down for me."
To Close with Conviction
"Here's what I'm committing to from my side—"
"My recommendation, and I own it, is—"
"The decision I'd make, with what we know now, is—"
"Let me summarize what I think we aligned on today."

The Daily Disciplines
1
Prepare one insight before every meeting
Before any conversation, formulate one observation or question that no one else in the room is likely to raise. One prepared insight outperforms an hour of improvised talking.
2
Never fill silence with noise
When you don't know what to say, say nothing. A composed pause reads as authority. Nervous filler reads as insecurity. Train yourself to be comfortable with 3 seconds of silence.
3
Replace "I think" with "My view is" or "My read on this is"
"I think" is hedged and soft. "My view is" or "My take" signals ownership without sounding arrogant. Small shift, measurable credibility gain.
4
Absorb one quality piece of long-form writing daily
At 20 books a year, you're already ahead of 95% of people. Supplement with one long-form essay or article daily — The Atlantic, Foreign Affairs, Stratechery. Vocabulary and frameworks compound.
5
Debrief one conversation per week
After a high-stakes conversation, spend 5 minutes asking: What landed? What fell flat? What would I say differently? Deliberate reflection accelerates growth faster than volume.
